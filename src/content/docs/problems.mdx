Las Vegas didn't end up the largest American metro without a single mile of light rail by accident. The Valley's transit deficit is the product of specific decisions made by specific actors over the past sixty years. Fixing it means understanding what we're up against.

## Built environment

The Valley grew explosively in the post-war auto era, and grew again through the 1990s and 2000s. Both booms happened during periods when American planning treated cars as the default and everything else as an afterthought:

- Arterials are six to eight lanes wide, designed to move cars at 45+ mph. They're hostile to walking, hostile to cycling, and hostile to bus operations.
- Most of the Valley's residential land is single-family zoning at densities too low to support frequent transit on their own.
- The freight rail network that runs through the Valley is owned and operated by Union Pacific. Sharing it with passenger service is technically possible but politically and operationally hard, the same way every Amtrak corridor west of Chicago is hard.
- We have no preserved transit right-of-way. Every BRT or rail project has to be carved out of existing road space — which means every project becomes a fight.

## Political economy

The Valley's land-use and transportation history is shaped by industries that benefit from the status quo:

- The casino and taxi lobby fought a direct rail connection between Harry Reid International and the Strip in the 1990s. Tourists who ride a train from the airport are tourists who don't ride a taxi or a Lyft, and aren't dropped at a specific casino's front door. That fight cost the region a generation of regional rail momentum.
- RTC's operating budget depends heavily on sales tax tied to the tourism economy. When tourism dips, transit funding dips with it — exactly the wrong direction.
- Governance is fragmented across the City of Las Vegas, North Las Vegas, Henderson, Boulder City, unincorporated Clark County, and the RTC. Ambitious regional projects get pulled apart by parochial interests at every step.

## Climate

It's hot here. People will tell you transit is unrealistic in the Valley because nobody wants to wait at a bus stop in 110-degree sun. The first half of that statement is true. The conclusion is wrong.

Phoenix and Dubai both run rail and BRT successfully in worse heat than ours. The fix is shaded shelters, frequent service so the wait is short, air-conditioned vehicles, and stop placement that doesn't strand riders a third of a mile from where they're going. The Maryland Parkway BRT is being designed with all of these in mind. Heat is not a reason to stop; it's a reason transit infrastructure has to be built carefully — which costs money, which is why funding matters.

## Funding

Transit in Nevada is structurally underfunded:

- The state's fuel revenue mechanism explicitly excludes transit. Every gallon of gas sold here funds roads. None of it funds buses.
- Federal transit dollars from IIJA and FTA programs require local match and local political consent. Without organized advocacy, that consent has been hard to come by.
- RTC has done remarkable work on the operating side — 24-hour service, hydrogen-fueled BRT, on-time performance better than most peer agencies — within a budget designed for a smaller city in a different decade.

None of these obstacles are unique to Las Vegas. Every Sun Belt metro that built a successful transit campaign — Phoenix, Albuquerque, Nashville, Austin — faced some version of all four. They won because someone organized.
