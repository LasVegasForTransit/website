In March 2026, the Regional Transportation Commission of Southern Nevada wrote to the Legislature with a warning: without a new dedicated funding source, **42% of bus service will be cut in 2028**. Fifteen routes eliminated. The 14th-busiest bus system in the country, hollowed out in a single budget cycle.

The Nevada Legislature meets every two years. The next session — February 2027 — is the only one before the cuts hit. There is no second window. If the 2027 session ends without a dedicated transit revenue source, recovery doesn't start in 2029. Recovery in 2029 starts from a smaller, weaker, more politically vulnerable baseline — service already cut, ridership already pushed off, riders already forced into cars they can't afford to keep.

This is what's at stake.

## The cliff is not abstract

When the RTC says 42% of service goes away, this is who pays:

- **Hospitality workers** who get to the Strip on a 2 AM bus from North Las Vegas because that's when their shift starts.
- **Students** at CSN and UNLV who already plan their schedules around the bus, because the parking situation isn't getting cheaper.
- **Seniors** who use Silver STAR paratransit funded out of the same pot.
- **Households** running one car instead of two, who would have to buy a second car they can't afford to feed.

Every one of them will be a pressure point on the rest of the system. The cars they're forced to put back on the road will worsen the congestion the rest of the Valley experiences. The household budgets they wreck will pull money out of the local economy. The wages they lose to missed shifts will land at food banks and emergency rooms instead.

## "We'll fix it later" doesn't work in transit

Transit service has a perverse loop: when it gets cut, ridership falls — not just on the cut routes, but across the whole network, because riders learn the system can't be trusted. When ridership falls, the political case for funding gets harder. When funding gets harder, more service gets cut. The Bay Area and Detroit have both lived through this loop. It is extremely hard to climb back out.

The corollary: it's far cheaper to defend service today than to rebuild it after a collapse.

## What "winning" 2027 looks like

A dedicated, recurring revenue stream for RTC operations and capital. Not a one-time bailout. Not a study. Something in the tax code that funds buses, the Maryland Parkway BRT, the Charleston corridor, and whatever comes next, the same way Nevada's fuel revenue funds roads.

The mechanisms exist. Phoenix did it with a county sales tax. Salt Lake County did it with a sales tax. Seattle did it with a regional sales tax that also bonds for capital. Los Angeles did it twice. Every one of those wins started with an organized constituency that legislators could not ignore.

Las Vegas does not have that constituency yet. The clock is the 2027 session.
